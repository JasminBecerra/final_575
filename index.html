Chicago Public High Schools: College Readiness

The state of Chicago Public Schools (CPS) has garnered attention in discussions on inequality in American public education. In early 2017, Mayor Rahm Emanuel proposed a new set of graduation requirements for CPS high school seniors. In particular, one additional requirement for high school seniors has resulted in backlash from the public:

proof of acceptance into college or the military, or a trade or a ‘gap-year’ program1

So, what does college readiness look like in Chicago?

**A NOTE ABOUT OUR DATA: We have selected an assortment of both district and charter schools to include on our interactive map. We used high schools that had 2016 data available for the majority of the college readiness indicators outlined in the purple menu panel.**

Overview

NETWORKS

Chicago Public Schools organizes its district-run schools into 13 networks. Networks are defined as geographic regions in the city. CPS does not group Charter, Contract, or SAFE /ALOP schools (“schools managed by independent operators for students who have been expelled from other schools due to violence” and “provide educational options for students who have dropped out of school and seek to return” respectively) into any of the 13 geographic networks.

Out of 172 high schools, 95 were district run for the 2016-2017 school year.

Because we are looking strictly at CPS’s 13 geographic networks, the data visualized in our map and elsewhere is restricted to only the schools that are categorized into a geographic network.

DISTRICT HIGH SCHOOLS

District high schools are schools operated by CPS. Our data includes the the following subcategories of district schools:

neighborhood schools - schools with “designated attendance boundaries"

career academies - college-prep and career-specific schools

magnet schools - schools that specialize in a “in a specific subject area such as Montesorri, International Baccalaureate, math and science, humanities or dual language immersion”

selective enrollment schools - schools that are “Designed to meet the needs of Chicago's most academically-advanced students,” requiring all applicants to complete an entrance exam.3

CHARTER HIGH SCHOOLS

In CPS, Charter schools are “approved by the Board of Education but operate independently” from both the Board and each other.

Charter schools each have different school calendars, schedules, and curriculums. While most district schools have attendance and enrollment boundaries, some charter schools do not.

Admission into a charter school varies by school. Some require applications and entrance exams. Others may require applicants to undergo a random lottery drawing (especially if the number of applicants far exceeds the number of open seats).4

Content

AVERAGE ACT SCORE

Our first college readiness indicator is Average ACT score. CPS high schools administer the ACT college assessment exam to all high school juniors. The ACT is broken down into four comprehensive subjects: Math, English, Reading, and Science. Possible scores range from 1 to 36.

Aside from the four subject categories, students are also given a composite score, or an average ACT score.

FREE/REDUCED LUNCH

CPS offers Free/Reduced lunch to students based on their parent’s income. Here, we use Free/Reduced lunch data as a sort of indicator of income/socio-economic status.

On the map, we are representing the percent of the school’s body that receives free or reduced lunch, using 2016 data.

DROPOUT RATE

Dropout rates are based on the percent of students who have dropped out of high school. Data is based on 2016 5-year cohorts.

GRADUATION RATE

Graduation rates are based on the percent of students who completed high school and earned a high school diploma. Data is based on 2016 5-year cohorts.

PERCENT COLLEGE ENROLLMENT

Percent college enrollment is based on the percent of students who enrolled in college out of all those who graduated. The most recent available data to us was that of 2015.
